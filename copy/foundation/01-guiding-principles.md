# 01 — Guiding Principles

The rules of the game. Every line of ZeroSpoils copy — a tweet, an App Store
subtitle, a push notification — passes through these. When two choices compete,
the principles break the tie.

---

### 1. Lead with the wallet. Win the heart with the planet.

Money is the door; the planet is the reason people stay and share. Cold copy
opens with what it saves *them* (cash, food, hassle). The environmental good is
the halo we earn once they're in — never the opening pitch to a stranger.

### 2. The enemy is forgetting — never the user.

We fight "out of sight, out of mind," the silent bin, the good intention that
slipped. We never imply the reader is wasteful, lazy, or irresponsible. They're
busy and human. We're on their side, against the problem.

### 3. Warm, human, and never preachy.

We sound like a sharp, friendly person — not a brand, an NGO, or a lecture.
Plain words. A little wit. Zero guilt. If a line feels like it belongs on a
poster in a school hallway, cut it.

### 4. Concise beats complete.

Say the one true thing and stop. We'd rather land a single sharp line than list
every feature. The sermon goes in the About page; the hook does the work.

### 5. Show the moment, don't describe the mission.

Concrete beats abstract. "The chicken you were going to cook Thursday" lands;
"reduce household food waste" doesn't. Lead with the lived moment — the full
fridge, the forgotten herbs, the receipt — and let the benefit follow.

### 6. Every claim earns its place.

Numbers are powerful and we use them — but only ones we can source and defend
(see `variables.md`). No invented stats, no vague "studies show." Specific and
true, or cut.

### 7. The copy is as frictionless as the product.

ZeroSpoils removes friction from the kitchen; our words remove friction from the
read. Short sentences. One idea per line. It should sound right read aloud —
that's a real test we run, not a metaphor.

---

> **Tie-breaker test:** if a draft is clever but cold, preachy but worthy, or
> complete but long — the principle wins, not the draft.
